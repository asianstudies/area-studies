Remembrances::

  • 'Remembering Barney Cohn (Bernard S. Cohn, 1928-2003),' Newsletter of the Center for South Asian Studies at the University of Virginia, vol. 18, no. 2, Spring 2004, p. 13-14. This reflection also appeared at the website, 20 April 2004.
  • 'Joseph Allen Stein, 1912-2001,' Newsletter of the Center for South Asian Studies at the University of Virginia, vol. 15, no. 1, 2001, Fall 2001, p. 10 and 13. . Among Joe Stein's many notable designs in India were the Indian International Centre, the Triveni Kala Sangam, and among the last of his work, the Habitat Centre.
  • 'Remembering Tessa (Tessa Bartholomeusz, 1958-2001),' Newsletter of the Center for South Asian Studies at the University of Virginia, vol. 15, no. 1, 2001, Fall 2001, pp. 11 and 6. Tessa was a student of buddhism and the author of Women Under the Bo Tree: Buddhist Nuns in Sri Lanka (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1994). She earned her PhD in Religious Studies at the University of Virginia in 1991, and was on the faculty of Florida State University at the time of her death.
  • 'Arvind Narayan Das (1948-2000) (Obituary of an Historian),' The Journal of Asian Studies, in (obituary of historian)(Obituary), vol. 59, no. 4, November 2000, p. 1103(2). . Arvind was an activist historian, journalist and one of the founder/editors of the review journal . Also announced at the website, 8 August 2000.
  • 'Qeyamuddin Ahmad (1930-1998) (Obituary),' Journal of Asian Studies (Ann Arbor, MI), vol. 58, no. 2, May 1999, p. 591-592. . Qeyamuddin Ahmad was on the history faculty of Patna University throughout his career. He was the author of The Wahabi Movement in India initially published in 1966 and reissued in a 2nd revised edition in 1994 by Manohar Publishers, New Delhi.
  • 'A. K. Ramanujan (1929-1993) (Obituary)'. The Independent (London), 31 July 1993, Saturday. Gazette Page, p. 45. Ramanujan was the poet and litterateur par excellence. He was Professor of Linguistics and Professor of South Asian Languages and Civlizations at the University of Chicago at the time of his death.
  • 'Gordon C. Roadarmel (1932-1972) (Obituary),' Journal of Asian Studies, vol. 31, no. 4, 1972, August 1972, p. 899. (With Warren F. Ilchman and David Rubin). Gordon had translated Premchand's Godaan for the UNESCO edition of the novel in 1968. His edited translation of modern Hindi short stories appeared as A Death in Delhi (Berkeley: University of California Press, 1972). Roadarmel was teaching at the University of California, Berkeley at the time of his death.
