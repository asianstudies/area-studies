Peter Robb, Ancient Rights and Future Comforts: Bihar, The Bengal Tenancy Act of 1885, and British Rule in India (London, Curzon Press), xxviii + 378 pp.

'Influence, in this country, goes with land, not with money; and it is influence that we want to preserve' (p.231). So noted Denzil Ibbetson, Secretary of the Revenue and Agriculture Department, at the turn of this century. As with many of Ibbetson's views, this one was straightforward and accurate. By the last half of the nineteenth century, it had become obvious to government that in order to continue their influence, they were going to have to overhaul, in dramatic fashion, the tenancy regulations in the Bengal Presidency. The question before them, then, was how to do so in a way that would secure them the greatest influence and at the same time shore up the legitimacy of their rule. It is this issue that Peter Robb addresses with admirable clarity and depth.

Ancient Rights and Future Comforts follows a tradition that originated with Eric Stokes' classic work, The English Utilitarinas and India (Oxford, 1959). That work, and others that followed it, provided an intellectual history of the monumental Permanent Settlement of 1793, which set up zamindars as proprietors of the land. Robb picks up this thread, and carries it over to the second-most important event in the history of land tenure in colonial Bengal: The Bengal Tenancy Act of 1885 (BTA). He writes an intellectual history of the Act of which Stokes would have been proud.

As with the earlier utilitarians, the administrators of the late nineteenth century felt an obligation to justify any changes in land tenure, basing them on a variety of issues. Of these, two were primary, and it is these two that Robb deals with extensively: Should the BTA be based on 'custom' or 'progress', and should the settlement be made with zamindars or raiyats (cultivators)? These two issues became entwined, and they provided the discourse around which the Act developed.

Within this debate, however, Robb notes two important caveats: The Act was a response to the Permanent Settlement, which itself was based on a false set of premises, and the Act reflected the growing power of the state. As such, while the BTA had an enormous impact on agricultualists, it did so in ways that were not necessarily anticipated.

The first problem was the perception of 'custom'. The attempt to 'restore ... earlier practices' (p. 108) centered around the interpretations of the purpose of the Permanent Settlement, and how that act had changed pre-British land tenure: '[C]entral to the tenancy debate was a closely-fought contest between pro-peasant radical conservatives and pro-landlord conservative radicals' (p. 9). Both sides attempted to claim history as their ally. This history, however, was largely irrelevant; '[I]t was the perception of need [for real change] that mattered' (p. 165).

Given that the BTA was, to a large extent, based on false assumptions, what in fact did it accomplish? Robb argues that in order to understand its impact, we must look at continuities in Indian history rather than disruptions. Particularly at the local level, while the BTA created new subsets of tenants, '... the state was not really present, in the maintenance of registers, to protect the weak. Rather it provided machinery to record and entrench the success of the strong' (p. 277). That the BTA tended to confirm the status of the powerful should not be surprising; Robb emphasizes the crucial role politics played in the formation of the Act. Publicity, job satisfaction, genuine concern about rights in land, and legitimacy, all were factors employed in making and remaking land tenure changes in Bengal (p. 370). Perhaps of most import, however, was the impact of a convoluted mixture of 'custom' and Eurocentrism: '... [T]he contribution of indigenous ideas was large and independent, but remained, in regard to state policy, one of establishing conditions upon which imported concepts had to work' (p. 332). In part because of this paradox, the Bengal Tenancy Act still has social and political ramifications at the local level in north India today.

Ancient Rights and Future Comforts is a dense book. This is not a criticism; it deals with a dense subject. As such, Robb deserves congratulations for his ability to imbue the reader with a sense of the purpose and results of the Bengal Tenacy Act that is not overwhelmed by specifics and obscurities. This book deserves a welcomed place alongside the earlier classic studies of agrarian and revenue law in colonial India.

Christopher V. Hill
University of Colorado
