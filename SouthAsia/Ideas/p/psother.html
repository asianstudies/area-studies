This essay reviews some of the new literature on the transition to sustainable rural development (SRD). By considering various accounts of environmental degradation, its links with poverty and aspects of the agenda for SRD, the essay notes an ambiguity regarding the role of the state, which is held, in this literature, as culpable for environmental degradation, as well as given a substantial role, implicitly or explicitly; in making the transition to SRD. This ambiguity is shown via an analysis of the treatments written from historical, socio-cultural and political economy perspectives, from which the essay draws the theme of state-class relations, arguing a central position for these relations in SRD r agendas. The essay concludes with an argument for a move to create a framework of analysis which takes into account not only public policy but also political economy and popular politics.

I.                 SUSTAINABLE RURAL DEVELOPMENT (SRD)

 

A global agenda for sustainable development has been in the making for at least the last quarter of a century. In that time, despite significant successes, the degradation of the natural environment has continued apace. The fact that ideas and their implementation have lagged behind the process they aimed to arrest, if not to reverse, has maintained a sense of urgency in the sustainable development literature, and with good reason. High levels of rural poverty persist, and substantial numbers (in some countries the majority) of people do not have secure access to food. With populations increasing, and political trends not moving towards a quick redistribution of wealth at the global and local levels, more rather than less food needs to be produced. Rural development programmes in developing countries, for a variety of reasons, have either failed, or had modest success, or, where they were more successful, have run against their own limits. In many cases, it is precisely where more food is needed more urgently that ecosystems seem at their most fragile and thus less capable of meeting projected increases in needs and demand.

 

Long-standing systems of sustainable agriculture, such as forest and bush fallow, wet rice cultivation and integrated crop-animal husbandry, have failed to keep up with rapid increases in human populations and incomes, Ruttan ('Sustainable Growth in Agricultural Production: Poetry, Politics and Science', in Vosti and Reardon) points out. He notes that the concern with sustainability is justified because developing countries will demand more food until the middle of the next century, due to growth in population and changing consumer demands and preferences. Future sources of productivity growth are not apparent, and while techno-fixes such as advances in molecular biology and genetic modification are advanced as solutions, the truth of their claims is currently being fiercely contested. In any case, the scarcity of phosphate fertilisers and fossil fuels will still remain a constraint. De Haen ('Environmental Consequences of Agricultural Growth in Developing Countries', in Vosti and Reardon) similarly argues that co-evolutionary processes are no longer adequate to

Intensification of agriculture for productivity growth requires improved seeds, fertilisers, irrigation, etc., but they cause loss of biodiversity and salinisation. This poses a question puzzling many of the authors under review: how to increase food production when current technologies have diminishing returns and increasing environmental costs?

 

There has been belated recognition of the fact that environment degradation and poverty are entwined in a downward spiral, each causing the other, and so sustainable rural development (SRD) has become important from the points of view of equity and social justice as well. As Buttel [1991] has pointed out, sustainability provides a disguised way of keeping social justice on the political agenda in a neo-conservative or neo-liberal world. Downing and Parry ('Global Climate Change: Implications for the Links between Sustainability, Growth and Poverty Alleviation', in Vosti and Reardon) point out that these issues acquire added urgency in the context of global climate change, which is expected to bring about changes in average rainfall, weather, extremes, and, via increases in carbon dioxide concentrations, on plant growth. This would make agriculture less predictable than it already is, and would likely exacerbate poverty. These trends and predictions establish the need for ideas and agendas for SRD.

 

It is because SRD claims to be more urgent and more fundamental an agenda than capitalist or socialist development - green as distinct from blue or red - that this article evaluates them as another agenda of transition altogether; asking if, indeed, the various agendas for SRD suggested by the writers avoid 'older' divisions. In the first part, I shall outline and analyse the agendas for SRD outlined by these authors, paying special attention to the forms of structural, institutional and technological changes they recommend, and the agents and interests they assume or argue will bring about these transformations. In the second part, I shall review the two historical monographs (Hill; Ninan) and a volume on state-society interactions over the natural environment (Madsen), to evaluate the various programmemes for SRD presented before. After discussing SRD from a public policy and then a political economy approach, I consider SRD as a possible political project, and suggest elements of a theoretical framework to analyse SRD.

The writings under review do not present a uniform narrative, though they do seem to share elements of populist renditions of environmental history, which have been identified by Greenough [1991: 2] as 'the standard environmental narrative' and as 'the new traditionalist discourse' by Sinha et al. [ 1997]. Chief among them is the argument of many of the writers that factors exogenous to the immediate context of resource use, such as the state and 'the larger economy', have primary responsibility for environmental degradation.

 

For example, Jodha, Shepherd and Tutweiler and Bailey all hold the external state responsible for degradation. For the Himalayan and Hindu Kush mountain areas Jodha ('Agricultural Growth and Sustainability: Perspectives and Experiences from the Himalayas', in Vosti and Reardon) argues that state interventions aimed to articulate the hills with national economies through physical infrastructure, market links and legal and administrative processes to extract 'mountain niche products' such as hydropower, timber and tourism services, but were marked by poor planning. Subsectors of the hill economy, such as irrigation, animal husbandry, forest use and agriculture, are treated as distinct from each other in government policy, but are inter-related on the ground. Plans emphasise food self-sufficiency, though livelihoods there historically have depended upon a more diverse portfolio of strategies including trade and transhumance. While his observations are accurate in their own right, he does not ask why hills are articulated with the national economies in the ways they are, what forms of power and sets of interests underlie such policies and plans, and if factors endogenous to the hills, such as population growth rates or structures of ownership and control over the forests on class or gender lines have any negative environmental outcomes.

 

Shepherd's critique of the Green Revolution (GR) as an example of the failure of state-led rural development, is more political than Jodha's. He points out that the technical intervention of the GR was done explicitly to avoid the politically more difficult course of redistributive land reforms. When implemented, GR exacerbated geographical inequalities between the few nations where it has been a success and those where it has been an abject failure, and, even where it has been more successful, between regions of success and failure. Shepherd argues that GR might have enhanced productivity in the short run, but increased poverty and inequality, and has had high environmental costs, ultimately failing on the twin tests of equity and sustainability. This reading of the GR is partial at best, since Shepherd does not consider the explicit justification for the GR, such as achieving rapid national self-sufficiency in food at a time when world food prices and availability were fluctuating wildly. A limited agency of livestock producers, as those who respond to state policies, forms the core of Tutweiler and Bailey's ('Agricultural Growth and Sustainability: Conditions for their Compatibility in the Rainfed Production Systems of West Asia and North Africa', in Vosti and Reardon) explanation for groundwater depletion, soil erosion and degradation in the West Asia-North Africa (WANA) region. Since the 1950s and 60s, states have subsidised feed supply to stimulate agricultural and livestock production to meet increased consumer demand fuelled by the oil boom, and at the same time imported food and feed. However, by the time the oil and regional labour markets ebbed in the 1980s, reducing government capacity to import food and feed, the area had not become self-sufficient in either. In the livestock sector, states now struggle to achieve a balance between feed supply and livestock numbers. Grazing lands face stress as herders use trucks to transport their herds, traversing in days the rangelands that earlier pastoralists took seasons to do. With the division of communal pastures into private property under land reforms, a tragedy of the commons like situation has been developing in the context of a heightened competition for feed.

 

This narrative assigns to the state almost total agency in policy formation, and indeed this may be the case in those countries where the state is authoritarian and isolated from social actors. But this notion of the state or of state-society relations is not made explicit in the narrative. If the converse is the case, and social groups do find access to policy formation, then the narrative would have to include an analysis of social power. Such an analysis of social power is attempted by Lindberg ('When the Wells Ran Dry: Tragedy of Collective Action among Farmers in South India', in Madsen), who shows in the case of Tamil Nadu that government price policies supported profligate water use by rich farmers leading to groundwater depletion, and that rich farmers as a class were collectively able to bear upon the government to maintain such policies for a considerable period of time. It would have been useful to know patterns of groundwater use by other rural classes, and if they too changed with the relations of these classes with the state, but this question lies outside the scope of Lindberg's study.

In South-east Asia, it is be important to know if farmers with more secure tenures actually undertake conservation.

 

A structure of perverse incentives at the level of the national economy is held by Reardon and Vosti ('Poverty-Environment Links in Rural Areas of Developing Countries', in Vosti and Reardon, eds.) as a cause of deforestation in Brazil's Amazonia province, where new settlers and forest capitalists have incentives to clear land, but not to invest in regeneration. These policy failures combine with market failures, such as an inadequate understanding and valuation of resources, producing a speculative rush on resources. This attention on incentives is important in its own right, but the question needs to be asked why settlers were sent to Amazonia in the first place, with its limited infrastructure, low soil productivity and lack of access, and what that says about their position in relation to the state and other claimants to land.

Some explanations of degradation point to factors over which the state has had only limited control. For the African semi-arid tropics, Webb and Coppock ('Prospects of Pastoralism in Semi-Arid Africa', in Vosti and Reardon) argue that pastoralism as a system is being eroded due to drought, population growth and territorial contraction, leading to a heightened conflict over resources. Population has risen faster than livestock numbers, which is constrained by forage availability, causing declines in per capita livestock holdings. As pastures are over-grazed milk production is reduced, the purchasing power of pastoralists deteriorates, and there is capital loss through livestock mortality. Territorial contraction has been the result of expansion of cropping, resulting in the loss of grazing lands. These factors interact with state initiatives such as irrigation schemes: most of Africa's irrigation schemes have been located in areas which provide dry season pastures. Facing heightened resource competition, many groups now commonly use dry season pastures so far reserved for use during droughts. This leaves them vulnerable during droughts, causing higher and faster mortality of livestock. In many cases grazing lands are going through formal enclosures. In this narrative, processes driving degradation are only marginally state-led, and pastoralist groups are only marginally in state control. In so far as degradation has primarily endogenous reasons, however, it is important to investigate if 'pastoralists' are homogenous groups and, if not, whether the behaviour of some sub-groups accelerates this process.

Also considering the international political economy context of environmental degradation, Siamwalla ('The Relationship between Trade and Environment, with Special Reference to Agriculture', in Vosti and Reardon) claims that trade and environment have no necessary relationship, and then shows how states make use of international policies to produce domestic economic and environmental outcomes. His case study shows that Thailand's use of EC pricing policies to export cassava for feed was a veritable rural development policy, and had a profound impact on Thailand's forest. A very easy crop to grow, cassava lifted many Thai peasants from poverty. Since the state's property rights in forests were not properly enforced, the expansion of the area under cassava absorbed population growth, but had the effect of accelerating deforestation. The cassava boom came to an end in the 1980s, when the limits of the arable were reached, and when EC set an upper limit on feed imports from Thailand. Similarly, Spencer and Polson ('Agricultural Growth and Sustainability: Conditions for their Compatibility in the Humid and Subhumid Tropics of Africa', in Vosti and Reardon) show that in Cote d'Ivoire, the development of export-oriented cash crops such as cocoa, coffee, plantains, palm oil and rubber has expanded the area under cultivation, as efforts to intensify production failed. This has caused deforestation, the gains from which have been short-lived and modest. While these examples are similar in that they both produced environmental degradation, it is notable that in the Thai case, cassava production did have a positive impact on poverty alleviation.

 

The degradation of the natural environment, in these essays, is thus attributed to (a) aspects of the international economy, namely asymmetrical relations between developed and developing countries and incentives and constraints this opens for developing country policy-makers and farmers; (b) the intended and unintended consequences of past state-led policies of accelerated economic growth, either directly or through the system of incentives they unleashed; and (c) factors such as population growth and density in interaction with local topography and eco-systemic specificities and wider political economic forces. I would stress that while these explanations are valid in some cases to some extent, a more comprehensive framework would incorporate the agency of states and social classes in creating and maintaining the articulations that produce environmental degradation.

III.             IMPACT OF RESOURCE DEPLETION ON POVERTY

 

Poverty and the poor are of central concern to many of the authors under review who draw links between environmental degradation and poverty. Lipton ('Accelerated Resource Degradation by Agriculture in Developing Countries? The Role of Population Change and Responses to It', in Vosti and Reardon) argues that population growth rates outstrip food production and, as labour surpluses are created, push down wages and food entitlements. Trying to escape or at least postpone Malthusian hell, the poor migrate to sparsely populated but environmentally fragile area, leading to their degradation. Jodha (op. cit.) argues that environmental degradation in the Himalayas and Hindukush makes it difficult to carry out fertility-regenerative farming and increases the food deficit period between seasons, forcing outmigration by the poor. Pingali ('Agriculture-Environment-Poverty Interactions in the Southeast Asian Humid Tropics', in Vosti and Reardon) notes that poverty in deforested areas of the Philippines is significantly higher than the national average. Whitaker et al. (op. cit.) likewise link decreasing per capita productivity of soil and water in the Indian semi-arid tropics and the fact that rural incomes are a fourth of the national average. Cunha and Sawyer (op. cit.) argue that policies such as expansion of cultivation in the rainforest have made at best a modest and short term contribution to poverty alleviation. It is to counter these trends that SRD agendas claim simultaneously to be poverty alleviation programs.

 

The arguments above tend to see the poor in isolation from the rest of rural society. The interplay between the poor and the rural rich is raised in Chakraborty's ('Linkages between Income Distribution and Environmental Degradation in Rural India', in Madsen.) discussion of the relations between environmental degradation and income distribution more generally. He posits a twofold relationship: first, he shows that income distributions are affected because degradation affects the poor more than the rich. Second, he shows that the rural rich are exposed to new opportunities to generate profits through resource use. The rural rich's access to resources has been enhanced by state policies, and they have been able to benefit most from the privatisation of communal grazing lands, and from new urban demands for milk and meat. Faced with reduced access to resources, the poor put pressure on already fragile commons. Government subsidies have aimed to encourage the rural poor to buy livestock and stimulate regional integration of milk markets, but a shrinking fodder base has caused increased pressures on grazing lands. Rich and poor in Chakraborty's scheme relate to each other in rural resource economies through three key structural asymmetries. First, the rich appropriate resources at the expense of the poor. Secondly, the rich are relatively stronger positioned in various

markets. Finally, as a smaller and better connected class, the rural rich have fewer obstacles to collective action. These asymmetries combine to produce insecurity of property rights for the rural poor. Their access to resources, or incentives to take a long view in their use, are both limited as a result. Also, as resource use forms only a small part of their portfolio of activities, minor degradation of the resource base affects the rural rich less severely than the poor. There are other relevant structural asymmetries, for example a unidirectional flow of externalities, in which richer and more powerful actors can inflict such externalities on weaker actors who cannot reciprocate the flow. Finally, there is asymmetry in terms of monitoring and sanctioning resource overuse.

 

Chakraborty presents a complex analysis of the relation between environmental degradation and poverty by focusing on aspects of structural power. But 'rich' and 'poor' are still ill-defined categories, and one would need to know how this intersects with other rural classes and social groups, including caste and gender. Another implication of Chakraborty's work, which I will consider later, is whether SRD agendas consider the range of structural asymmetries that produce poverty and environmental degradation. Also, Chakraborty does not tell us if political projects of reversing power relations in the countryside are possible given that the poor are structurally less able to organise collective action, and where already ongoing, if they have had a positive environmental outcome.

 

A further attempt to disaggregate 'poverty' is by Reardon and Vosti ('Poverty-Environment Links in Rural Areas of Developing Countries', in Vosti and Reardon) who are dissatisfied with the exclusive concern with welfare-oriented measures pervasive in the literature. They argue that different types of poverty reflect the lack of specific assets, and therefore have specific impact on the environment. Likewise, they advocate a typology of environmental change, since each type would have a different relationship with poverty. In this connection, they suggest that poverty measurements accommodate the notion of 'conservation-investment poverty'. They find welfare oriented measures of poverty inadequate, since households which are not poor on these criteria might still be too poor to make conservation investments. Second, welfare poverty looks at individuals or households, not 'communities' that, according to Reardon and Vosti are the ideal units to undertake such investments. Further, they ask if poverty alleviation is adequate to redress environmental problems. Reardon and Vosti advance a new conceptual framework that includes categories of assets held by the rural poor, important especially if there is limited substitutability of assets. While the suggestion regarding typologies of environmental change and categories of assets is an interesting one, especially from the point of view of making targeted policy interventions, it

Cunha and Sawyer (op. cit) present an explanation of the relations between regional poverty and environmental degradation. Even if it were able to measure it, it is not clear how useful it would be given that Amazonia's poverty is a result of its articulation with Brazilian political economy. The concept does not tell us how new investment in conservation could be brought into a region with low demand and low state capacity and interest to stimulate it.

IV.            THE AGENDA FOR SUSTAINABLE RURAL DEVELOPMENT

 

So far, I have noted that the writings under review collectively argue that environmental degradation occurs due primarily to external agents such as the state or the larger economy, that such degradation exacerbates poverty and is in turn exacerbated by it. Since SRD agendas claim to address both degradation and poverty simultaneously, in this section I will first outline some of the agendas under review and then evaluate them on these criteria. Also, since I have argued that the question of agency - of the state, international actors, classes and other social groups - has been largely neglected, I will then highlight the implicit theories of agency embedded in these agendas.

Let us consider some definitions. In Vosti and Reardon's ('Introduction: The Critical Triangle of Links among Sustainability, Growth and Poverty Alleviation', in Vosti and Reardon) rather minimal definition, SRD implies compatibility between agricultural growth and conservation of the farmland natural resource base and of the commons; issues of redistribution or equity are not central. For Jodha (op. cit.), sustainability is the ability of a system to maintain a level of performance, say for output, over time and to enhance the same if necessary: the concern again is first with the productive capacity of the ecosystem. Concerned with pastoral systems, Webb and Coppock (op. cit.) take sustainability to mean both promoting food security and long term capacity of the resource base to support pastoral activities, the first concern justified on equity grounds, the second less so in light of their own review of the relations between pastoralism and degradation. Chopra and Rao's definition, that of successful management of agriculture to simultaneously meet changing human needs, reduce poverty and maintain or enhance the quality of the environment, correctly puts equal emphasis on growth, redistribution and ecological sustainability. The most extensive definition of SRD is Shepherd's, for whom, unlike the 'steady-state' arguments of previous decades, the new SRD agenda is not about zero growth, but about the quality of growth, implying ecologically sound, economically viable, socially just, humane and adaptable agricultural systems, based on low external inputs, indigenous knowledge and management, and using resource conserving and enhancing technologies. As this sample of definitions indicates, at their core SRD definitions are based on a balance between the productive and regenerative capacity of resources, to be achieved to meet equity-oriented goals such as food security and poverty alleviation, using participatory institutional and appropriate technological innovations. It should be noted that there is no 'natural affinity' between these goals; if anything, there are potential conflicts between one element and the next. How do SRD agendas propose to address them?

 

The International Policy Environment

 

Shepherd is the most favourably disposed of the authors under review to key elements of the new international policy agenda, such as structural adjustment programmes (SAPs) and trade liberalisation. He argues that SAPS have much to offer rural societies and economies by reducing state intervention which tie them in red tape, tax them excessively without providing enough in return, and are 'urban biased'. SAPS for him can reduce the power of elites who use the state to meet specific ends. He sees currency devaluation and subsidies reduction creating better prices for third world farm exports and making technology and equipment import more expensive, thus favouring employment and sustainability. that the post-Uruguay round trade scenario should provide lucrative incentives to move towards organic agriculture for environmentally aware farmers in developing countries that export agricultural goods, though he admits that the only winners yet are producers of goods so far suffering from high tariffs.

 

It is curious that Shepherd holds faith in these elements of the new policy agenda when he himself presents clear evidence against such optimistic prognostications. He reviews the few full studies of the environmental impact of SAPS which indicate that they encourage environmental degradation directly and indirectly. They tend to increase uncertainty for farmers, few of whom can change fixed factors at short notice, say when subsidies are withdrawn. Also, export oriented policy changes benefit agriculture only when export markets exist, and producers have some control over prices, which is not the case currently. Instead of being alleviated, poverty is exacerbated by reduced government expenditure in the short and medium term, forcing the poor to environment degrading livelihood practices. Shepherd is aware that the poor lack the endowments to make use of the opportunities these changes open, and recommends that new strategies include temporary safety nets and address the root causes of food insecurity, as well as plans to remove distortions in the public sector, encourage the private sector, diversify the agricultural base, and reduce dependence on a small number of export products. One problem with this persistence with advocacy of the new agenda in the face of evidence to the contrary is that it contains no direct means of achieving the goals which have been placed at the centre of his definition of SRD. Additionally, there is no consideration of the negative impact of state shrinkage on achieving SRD's objectives.

 

Shepherd's other argument that international policy requirements such as a reduction in subsidies will act as a disincentive to the use of chemical inputs and fuel, and thus encourage sustainable agriculture, is contradicted by Malton and Adesina ('Agricultural Growth and Sustainability: Prospects for Semi-Arid West Africa', in Vosti and Reardon) who argue that the decline in the use of chemical inputs can have short-term negative effects on productivity and poverty. In the west African semi-arid tropics, the soil would likely have enhanced productivity with increased fertiliser use, since it chiefly suffers from phosphorous and nitrogen deficiencies. However, market forces have increased prices just as SAPS have led to cuts in subsidies. Malton and Adesina recommend an alternative fertiliser management practice, cheaper, more efficient and economically sustainable, but the policy environment seems to work against it since it takes the state out of precisely those sorts of roles. The question of the political basis for such a change in the role of the state will be taken up at the end of this essay.

Technological and Institutional Change

 

No uniform combination of technological and institutional innovations has been offered by the authors under review, though they form the larger part of the recommendations for achieving SRD. Again, Shepherd provides the most comprehensive list of requirements these innovations must meet: they must improve productivity of labour, capital and land without degrading the natural resource base. These must be adaptable by small farmers, not requiring radical changes by them, nor heavy initial investments. To avoid the failure of past attempts at technology transfers, new technologies must be developed with the maximum participation by farmers, including women. Let us review some cases of such innovations and then consider some problems in achieving these goals.

 

Interestingly, the move towards integrated pest management (IPM) currently finding favour in development circles, would not meet Shepherd's criteria for SRD. IPM is advanced as a panacea for many of the problems associated with chemical inputs, but as Shepherd correctly notes it requires a radical redesign of the entire farming system. Farmers need to be able to predict the outbreak of diseases, consider pest management in making other on-farm decisions, develop a better understanding of pest ecology and population dynamics, as well as be able to calculate when to switch to chemical controls in the event of continued damage. This signals potential problems in the adoption of technological change.

 

The preference for institutions under 'local' control is strong in many of the recommended innovations. Malton and Adesina suggest new institutions such as the smaller scale village level irrigation systems in the Sahel with more responsibilites to individuals and farmer groups. While this move from larger to smaller scale systems is laudable from the perspective of participation, the authors do not mention how local groups coordinated with each other in water use, and whether the enabling conditions prevailing in the Sahel are similar to those elsewhere. Webb and Coppock note that after many decades when Hardin's 'tragedy of the commons' held sway over policy in Zimbabwe, there is new emphasis on the creation of herders' associations and grazing groups that regulate stock trees, rotational grazing and overall rangeland use. An account of how such collective action came about is missing in these case studies, and the question of agency is not explicitly addressed.

V.              THE STATE AND THE NATURAL ENVIRONMENT: HISTORICAL PERSPECTIVES

 

I have raised the issue of political economic constraints on public policy, referring mainly to the ways in which relations between classes, and between them and the state, act to produce environmental degradation and to thwart the implementation of recommendations for technological and institutional innovations. As noted above, while narratives of decline are often state-centric, many SRD agendas still require a considerable role for the state. The question this raises is: why should the state, so far an agent of degradation, now become a handmaiden to SRD? To address this issue of changes in the nature of the state and in state interests, I now put the prospects of future transitions against the evidence of past histories. I will specifically address the ways in which state-class relations affected resource use in the cases of South Asia and Japan which are explicitly dealt with in Ninan, Hill and Madsen.

 

States, Classes and Natural Resources in South Asia

 

In his excellent study of colonial Purnia district in north India, Hill suggests that the subordination and transformation of nature and the instituting of private property were key ideological underpinnings of the colonial state, and, through the system of permanent settlements, a key source of revenue as well. The chief problem for the state in Purnia stemmed from its location on the Kosi river, known to be an erratic, violently flooding, meandering river, causing huge instability in agriculture: in a hundred years, the river shifted course a total of 73 kilometres, and not in any one direction. The state attempted to survey the river and riparian land so as to design effective

Settling the Kosi area was a problem, since frequent flooding on the one hand created diara, temporary or new land, and, on the other, also carried sand on cultivable land. In this dismal place, another problem was to maintain a labour supply for agriculture, involving the formulation of suitable immigration policies as well.

 

Frustrated by the river, the state also failed to intervene decisively in local class relations. Zamindars, landlords recognised in permanent revenue settlements, had responded to revenue demands by increasing rents and frequently deployed physically coercive means on tenants for collecting their dues. They made use of ecological and institutional uncertainties to extract more rent: a common ploy was to keep tenants under uncertainty by delaying the renewal of leases. State legislation could afford no protection to tenants as long as the riparian lands remained unsurveyed. Despite its intentions, the Bengal Alluvion and Diluvion Act of 1847, while providing abatements to those whose lands were covered by flood sand, made no provision for those whose lands were washed away.

 

Hill notes that this disease, swamp and wildlife infested land was a 'punishment posting' for the bureaucracy, which was therefore staffed by corrupt and inept officers. Since most administrators wanted quickly to be posted out of there, there was little continuity in the local state which, Hill points out, was never more than a conspicuous presence. Few in local administration had knowledge of the local conditions, and their ineptitude and ignorance was used productively by those with local power to maintain and extend their control over the peasantry. Rampant corruption in the local state, trans-border crime, and Purnia's involvement in the political tussles taking place in Nepal further reduced state capacity. When attempts were made to extend control, such as the Anglo-Nepal war of 1812, local landlords were not supportive of the colonialists. In fact, Gurkhas were able to cross into Purnia, and cut and carry standing crops, dealing a heavy blow both to British morale and to revenue. Moreover, since many dacoits and landlords had a symbiotic relationship, the latter did little to assist the local state in their capture.

 

The colonial state, in Hill's account, also had incomplete control over agrarian class relations, and was unable to rupture the exploitative relations landlords had with their tenants and labourers. Landlords seldom lived up to their responsibilities to the state of clearing jungle land and eradicating wild animals to promote cultivation. State efforts to get more lands cleared for cultivation, for example by giving out land tracts to invalids from the army failed. Invalids could not work the land which was repossessed, then auctioned by district administration, mostly to absentee landlords.

While the explanation of groundwater depletion is tied to the relations between the state and rich farmers, Lindberg does not address the issue of changes in water use as state actors made new alliances with other agrarian classes.

 

Knudsen ('Deforestation and Entrepreneurship in the North-West Frontier Province', in Madsen) points to the incomplete control states have over the natural environment, pointing out that the fines for forest offences listed in Pakistan's forestry legislation are too low to be a deterrent. Working plans set harvesting targets which are too high, resulting in low regeneration. Even guzara forests which are sometimes listed as communal are only under the nominal control of the community. Crude felling methods which cause considerable collateral damage are tolerated. Instead of doing away with forest contracts as a system, the state replaced contractors with the Forest Development Corporation, which continued to sublease to contractors. Knudsen does not specify if, at the core of deforestation, it is the state's lack of technical knowledge or of capacity, or whether contractors are part of the class basis of the state, and therefore can flout rules with impunity regardless of environmental outcomes.

 

These studies of state-society interactions in South Asia show that the local capacity of the state varies in terms of control of resources. While in Gold's case this variation is explained by personal attributes of rulers, the other examples clearly show that the chief explanation of differing state control over nature lies in varying state relations with classes and other social groups. It is thus crucial for SRD agendas to address state-society relations.

 

The State, Class Relations and Forests in Japan

 

Ninan's account of Japanese forestry reveals a different type of state than the variants of the south Asian state discussed above. The state in Japan seems much stronger and more capable of achieving its objectives. From the sixth century onwards, the state had led the demand for timber for the construction of capital cities, castle towns, temples, shrines, monuments, etc. State ownership of land was established in the eightth century, allowing

landowners lifelong usufruct but no transfer rights. New rights were created in the twelvfth century for those who opened up woodland, which later developed into hereditary rights. Facing low land availability, the state encouraged land reclamation, giving ownership rights to those who reclaimed it. These rights of the state to forests were balanced with a sense of prudence in forest use. From the eghth century onwards, the state passed decrees against overuse of timber, in some cases to protect water sources.

 

Forests were also a direct source of state revenue. Taxes were charged on village and private forests, and an officer appointed explicitly for this purpose. The multiple uses of the forests themselves made more revenue available: there were taxes on collecting firewood, fodder, green manure and timber, plus for making charcoal. State collection of revenue from rice cultivation also had an impact on forest use, through the expansion of the arable by felling forests, the use of sugi wood in terracing, and the use of green manures.

 

Increasing warfare in the late Tokugawa period pushed up the demand for metals and metallurgy, requiring timber to fire the smelters. Fuelwood and charcoal were also required for the production of sea salt. The growing importance of forests was matched by increasing concern for them, and forest overseers were appointed. This concern with timber prompted the state to extend its power and control over river navigation to regulate the timber trade. To control the supply of lumber, the state initiated regulation of lumber markets, requiring traders to obtain contracts and authorisation. In forests where logging was permitted, new restrictions were placed on the quantum of cutting, as well as on species mix, with permission required for the felling of key trees.

 

Feudal governments also controlled local village forests, closing access to parts of them for regeneration of certain species. They required village communities to report on forest stocks on the basis of which to make forest policy. There were strict penalties for non-compliance: one village in an area was made to monitor forest use by others, and was paid for this. If the village failed, they had to pay for the damage, sometimes twice the market rate for that sort of timber. Sometimes, death penalties were imposed on forest offenders. Apart from these institutional changes, two key technological innovations were introduced in the eighteenth century: rotational cutting, and twenty year cutting cycles in coniferous trees were both aimed at providing sustainable harvests.

 

Compulsory afforestation activities were introduced by feudal lords in the eighteenth century, with farmers' tree cutting privileges (not rights) linked to tree planting, some fiefs even specifying the tree to be replanted, as well as peasants' responsibilities for planting and tending to saplings. Those peasants not complying with new rules risked forfeiture of cutting

rights. Institutional changes included the introduction of contract forestry, in which village communities provided land and labour, merchants the capital. Another institutional innovation, in which government or private lands were leased to villagers for afforestation with proceeds shared according to the terms of contract, became widespread by the end of the eighteenth century. Also, in some cases, village community or government lands were divided among households to create new individual incentives for afforestation. Together these changes caused a move from unregulated exploitative forestry at the beginning of the seventeenth century. to extensively organised plantation forestry by the end of the eighteenth century. These changes supported the coinciding interests of two dominant classes, feudal lords and merchants, and also responded to peasant rebellions against restricted access to forests.

 

The Meiji restoration of the nineteenth century explicitly aimed to make Japan into a modern industrial capitalist country, so forests were articulated with other sectors of the national economy in new ways. A centralised, modernizing state abolished and took over feudal control over forests partly also to undermine feudal power. Forest taxes were simplified and regularised across fiefs, and made payable in cash. A new classification of forests was introduced, and 'official forests' expanded to include those forests used only for gathering, without any spending of other labour or capital in their upkeep. Municipalities as new administrative units took over many hamlet forests. Government forest land selling schemes transferred forests to landlords and timber merchants. Also, since new taxes were levied on private forests many peasants sold them to other private owners, resulting in the concentration of forest holdings.

 

In addition new Forest, River and Erosion Laws, jointly known as the Three Laws of Flood Control were introduced, tying forest use to conservation objectives. New 'protection forests' were created, protecting from all sorts of 'natural' disasters. A Forest Police was empowered to evict encroachers and farmers illegally on national forest land. The Japan Timber Standards of 1926 was designed to cut down transaction costs in timber trade by simplifying the classification of marketable timber. Under new rules village forest boundaries were demarcated anew, but community rights were so seriously affected that in the 1890s there was a series of protests throughout the country leading to arson and looting, neglect of afforestation, and in extreme cases to burning of imperial and national forests. As a result, new proceeds-sharing institutions were introduced.

 

Thus forests now became a sector of the modern Japanese economy. The state invested in basic infrastructure such as roads, railways, sawmills, etc., and used the extensive reserve of silvicultural knowledge to create a new 'scientific forestry'. As a result of these far-reaching changes, Japan's

natural forests increasingly became 'man-made', coppice forests became high value-timber growing forests. In other words, as the state changed and reflected new constellations of interests, the nature of forests as a resource itself changed. The state attempted also to motivate non-state forests to follow its lead. The Forest Law of 1907 required large private forest owners to make forest use plans.

 

In 1920 the state promoted afforestation in municipal forests by providing credit and proceeds-sharing. Forest cooperatives were set up in the 1920s as a medium through which to transmit state priorities, and subsidies offered to those co-operatives which supported the flood control plans. In the period after the Second World War, forests were exempted from the extensive land reforms were passed under American occupation, despite support from farmers' association. They had been fiercely opposed by forest landlords, the ministry of agriculture and capitalists. Two justifications were offered: one that tenancy relationships in forestry were not as politicised as in agriculture, and two that the shift from the regime of private ownership might make protection arrangements uncertain, especially for flood and erosion control. Incentives were also given for afforestation, in the forms of proceeds sharing between tree planters and land owners. However, most of the afforestation took place in small holdings, as the beneficiaries of land reforms invested farm surpluses in silvicultural activities.

 

In the Japanese case as compared to South Asia, state ownership and control over forests seems to have been more total. States were involved in directing forest use both towards conservation and revenue maximisation, with the result being not so much deforestation as a changes in the species mix. The state also was the source of most of the institutional-technological innovations, such as land rights to those who cleared it (in contrast to Purnia), licences for timber trade, contracts, and even the death penalty to function as a negative incentive. State-led afforestation measures were responsive to peasant rebellions and included profit-sharing arrangements. The class nature of the state is evident from Ninan's forest history: at each stage of state formation, ascendant classes were able to use the state as an instrument to weaken previously dominant classes. The evidence of a strong and repressive state following revenue maximisation and, later, capitalist development, while managing to avoid deforestation, once again raises the question of the political-economic forms of the transition to SRD.

 

As this discussion of the state-class relationships shows, patterns of resource use are functions of relations between classes and other social groups, and between them and the state. It could be said that a stronger state is more effective in conserving and protecting the natural environment that a weak state, but this statement also is problematic, since state capacity varies over time, space and issue area. States are not able on their own to decommodify aspects of nature, and are unsuccessful in removing them from circulation in the market, as illegal trade in flora and fauna worldwide has shown. Centralised control does not translate to the everyday acts of monitoring and sanctioning, of surveying and stock-taking that state actors pursuing conservationist and preservationist agendas need to perform. Stated intent in international fora does not eradicate rampant corruption at lower levels of the state. In the event that the centralised state has asserted its authority, such as in the case of the wildlife sanctuaries in the Indian north-east or wetlands in the north, there have been backlashes ranging from local mobilisations to nationalist and subnationalist demands. States seem to have fragile, or at least contested, authority, incomplete knowledge and control over factors that militate against prudent resource use, shrinking size and financial base under SAP, and to be under the control of dominant groups and classes.

 

Public policy recommendations made by several of the authors outlined earlier seem likely to be frustrated by concerns of political economy and power relations discussed above. A key question in considering SRD as a political project is how the necessity for the state can be reconciled with its declining, uncertain and contested powers. Another, given that the only generalisable conclusion so far has been that state policies and degrees of success in their implementation are tied to class relations and the power of social groups, is to delineate the sorts of politics that would tend to move state action and capacity towards the SRD agenda.

VI.            SRD AS AN ALTERNATIVE FRAMEWORK AND POLITICAL PROJECT

 

It is in the sense that politics involves the movement from 'what is' to 'what ought to be' [Gramsci, 1971: 172] that I consider the frameworks on offer in analysing SRD as a political project. More concretely, I ask what sorts of politics, involving what kinds of agents and alliances would bring about the changes in the international policy environment, in the nature, capacity and interests of states, in the agency of actors and in development thinking and practice to achieve SRD.

 

Shepherd's own evidence and that of other writers do not justify the optimistic view that the new international policy environment is favourable to SRD. Recall Lipton's point that SRD would depend on changes in OECD farm, pricing and import policies. SRD as a political process would require

actors in developing countries to effect domestic policy changes within these countries, but there is no evidence to show that such agency is possible within given political institutions. Where fora exist to give developing countries a voice in international trade, such as at the WTO, the North American argument that environmental concerns are barriers to free trade has won the day, posing serious constraints on the achievement of SRD. The patenting by American and European companies of genetic materials and knowledge in plants grown in the developing world has raised the spectre of biopiracy. SAPS, which Shepherd lauds for favouring SRD, are not just neutral instruments, but have a more direct negative effect on the poor, and stabilisation packages are roundly despised in many countries as symbols of global inequity. While the authors under review are concerned with food security and poverty alleviation, the Rome Conference refused to recognise the right to food as a fundamental human right. Further, if one sees the progress made in the post-Rio period, the expenditure and concerns of the Global Environmental Facility clearly reflect the priorities of powerful countries. Livelihoods in arid and semi-arid lands has been a major concern for many of the writers under review, but a global conference, the current yardstick of the seriousness with which issues are considered in international environmental diplomacy, has not yet taken place on desertification. Alternative frameworks and political projects for achieving SRD therefore must address the structures of international political economy directly.

 

New global-national articulation of resource economies must be another focus of understanding and political action. By this I mean the ways in which, facing opportunities or constraints from the global economy, states change their natural resource use policies. This includes legal-administrative changes, such of those in Mexico where the state had planned to scrap the constitutional provision for redistribution of land to communal holdings, or those of Indian states which are passing a flurry of 'land acquisitions acts' which would make it easier to use agricultural land for non-agricultural purposes, seen as a bait for foreign investors. Contrary to Shepherd's claim that the new international policy agenda favours SRD, it would seem from these examples that it favours specifically capitalist development, with sustainability a goal only accidentally met if at all. Another type of relationship worth understanding is the one between the Costa Rican parastatal agency INBio and Merck, in which Merck funds conservation in return for potential monopoly rights over Costa Rican biodiversity and indigenous knowledge about it [Meyer, 19961. This case again reveals the chances and limits of achieving sustainability within capitalist development.

 

There have, of course, been global campaigns and coalitions for the sorts

The recent 'farmers' caravans' that traveled through Europe represented coalitions that included farmers from developing and developed countries and protested against the increasing corporate hold over genetic material such as seeds, though it is not clear if they also represented all classes of rural producers. The World Federation of Fishworkers likewise has had success in changing fishing laws in many countries by linking local with national and global collective action. Similarly, the global coalition against the Multi-lateral Agreement on Investment, arguably against the interests of developing countries by providing unhindered mobility of capital, was able to get it off the agenda. SRD frameworks and political projects must consider what makes such coalitions possible and what accounts for their success.

 

At the next level, SRD seems to require a fundamental change in the nature of state interventions in resource use. As the historical perspectives have shown, such state action usually does not have conservation as its main aims, and when it does, it is not concerned with poverty alleviation. SRD needs an environmentally enlightened state which acts competently in the interest of the poor. This leads to two sets of questions: what leads the state to reach environmental enlightenment, and what would it take to have the state act consistently in the interests of the poor? As to the first question works such as Grove [1995] seem to suggest that a major reason might be powerful scientists responding to and operating within academic networks, but not that involved with the political economy of resource use. Clearly, that relationship between state and science cannot be replicated in many developing countries today, faced as they are with institutional collapse or at least decline. Besides, the entire model of science as a separate from society is under attack, not least from votaries of participatory sustainable development. Another way could be for states to learn from enlightened international agencies and NGOs, but there is considerable opinion that this flow of funds and ideas is uni-directional and reflects the interests of donors and experts rather than some objective agenda, nor is the process without tension.

 

The question of state competence or capacity is one which has not been addressed by most of the essays making policy recommendations. The state's capacity, for example, to subsidise conservation investment, assumes that there is no opportunity cost to such expenditure. Likewise, the recommendation that new land taxes be imposed on the rural rich assumes state autonomy from them, or state willingness to act against the interest of one of its key allies. The 'strong hands, weak fingers' analogy that Herring refers to points to a structural problem with state capacity in terms of enforcement. The sort of state that can impose an ecologically and socially sustainable agenda would require autonomy from ruling classes, and

alliances with the poor. This would require a change in the nature of the state in most developing countries, and a realignment of social forces. How such coalitions can come about and what their relationship would be with other social groups and the state must be of central concern in SRD agendas.

 

As for the relations between the state and the poor and marginalised, the evidence is not strong that states have kept the interests of the latter at the core of their policies, that they have had much success, or where they have had success, it has come about through democratic means. Some examples are on offer of the politics of subordinate groups, but it is not clear that sustainability is at their core. Hill discusses a sense of lurking popular violence against the existing arrangements of access to and use of resources. From joining the national movement and the Kisan Sabha, Santhals and other Purnia tenants, sharecroppers and landless labourers by the 1960s had moved towards Maoism through the Naxalite movement in pursuit of some resolution of the land question. When the state asserted its authority by creating and then policing wildlife reserves, Herring shows that the Bodoland movement in the Indian north-east took up arms against the state since this meant their access to lands and forests were going to be curtailed. Ninan points out for Japan that large-scale rebellions by the peasantry against restrictions on their access to forests were usually followed by institutional change, such as proceeds-sharing contracts. SRD theoretical frameworks and political projects must take into account the ways in which mass politics of this kind changes the relations between social groups and the state, and sometimes leads to institutional change. As essays in Brass [1995] demonstrate, farmers' movements in countries such as India want more rather than less subsidies, and more environmentally damaging inputs to pursue capitalist agriculture. SRD agendas should be cautious in equating rural political movements with environmentally positive outcomes.

 

SRD agendas in many cases rely heavily on 'local community participation', and this requires an analysis of the full political significance of these terms. The cases reviewed here, such as those of Purnia, Punjab and Pakistan for example, show that 'communities' were unequal and characterised by exploitative relations. If the empowerment requirement of the more extensive SRD agendas is to be met, a reformulation of the community would itself have to be a prerequisite. SRD frameworks should include processes including social movements through which communities are altered, or through which new collectivities are formed. Cases of user-groups successfully managing resources must likewise unravel the articulations between them and the state. Likewise, 'participation' is potentially a deeply political process, since it aims to transfer the control over resources and decision-making to groups which have so far been excluded from such control. But it is not clear from the essays what the unit of

participation should be. The use of 'households' in most of the essays in Vosti and Reardon persists despite decades of research on gender within the household. In other cases, 'local communities' are offered without discussing its internal configurations. There are of course positive examples. Gooch provides details of the community forest management plan put forward by the van gujjars, which highlight their intimate knowledge and long history of interaction with the forests and wildlife to make claims to rights in the Rajaji National Park. They assert that they too are of the forest, and want training and employment in the park so that they can become involved in curbing illegal activities in the forest and in forest regeneration. Their blueprint for democratic control over the forest and of institutional cooperation with the Park authorities has received wide acclaim. But in other cases, treating households and communities as a whole can hide and exacerbate domination based on class, caste gender and age lines. Besides, as Ghai and Vivian [1992] point out, it is relevant to ask whose project communities are participating in, and whether they share the SRD agenda in whose implementation and design they are encouraged to participate.

 

Shepherd suggests 'new professionals' and 'development workers' who will work in radically-new ways, such as PRA, to interact with 'local communities', and take them through the legal system to pursue their rights and environmental sustainability. Certainly, there are numerous examples of NGOs that have done exemplary work in this regard. But there is no generalisable conclusion regarding their role, much less about their relationship with the groups on whose behalf they work. Secondly, there is no discussion on the motivations of these agents. When the emphasis of new institutionalism is so much on the incentives created by new institutions for poverty alleviation and environmental sustainability, it is hard to imagine what incentives these agents might have. As for technical change, including finding location-specific solutions, it is equally hard to imagine how existing institutions can carry out the requisite research in a period of low expenditure on agricultural research, or where new institutions will come from.

 

The articles under review do have a sense of the sorts of changes that would facilitate SRD, but the priority of policy over politics is ultimately frustrating to the agendas themselves. The question is, will the sorts of institutional and technological changes recommended here lead to the structural changes needed for the transition to SRI)? One would have to answer that question in the negative; indeed, many of the recommendations would need structural changes as prerequisites for achieving desired results. Further research should focus on the formation of collective identities, the changing articulations between social groups, states and the international system, and the process by which an agenda for policy reform becomes a mass demand for political transformation.
