The Eve of the British Conquest - I
Egypt, Mesopotamia, China and India amongst the most ancient civilisations in the World.
West Asia (Judaism, Christianity & Islam) and India (Buddhism & Hinduism) the original homes of the most popular religions in the World today.
Politically, empires alternated with fragmented Kingdoms throughout history.
Great cultural, religious, racial and linguistic diversity because of repeated migrations and conquests.
Socially, a three-tiered hierarchical society on account of the “caste system” : of great antiquity, but arguable flexibility.

The Eve of the British Conquest - II
Economically, India ahead of Europe before the Industrial Revolution, thus attracting a constant stream of visitors and traders from Asia, Europe and the Mediterranean world. Columbus actually searching for India when he discovered America!
Major exporter of spices, fine fabrics, perfumes, jewels (ivory, diamonds, other stones) dyestuffs (indigo and lac) and animals.
Agricultural productivity much higher than pre-agricultural revolution Europe: hence birth rates and population densities always higher.
Level of monetisation in the economy quite advanced.
Balance of trade always  unfavourable to the West, as India imported little apart from gold and pottery, leading Pliny to complain of the severe drain of gold from the Roman empire!

The British (“Colonial”) Impact - I
European Powers came to India in the eighteenth century in pursuit of trade, but on account of wide technological differences, the flag followed trade.
The handicraft/artisan based Indian industry could not compete with industrial goods:  Free trade imperialism and "deindustrialisation”.
India falls decisively behind Europe.
The foundations of a modern infrastructure: all-weather roads, railways and irrigation after 1860.
Replacement of a complex, custom based,  produce-sharing agrarian tax system by a modern, justiciable, written-record based, inflexible monetised tax administration.

The British (“Colonial”) Impact - II
Socially conservative policy, especially after the failed insurrection of 1857, but
The old landed class deprived of political and military authority: transformed from lords of the land into landlords.
The strengthened agrarian infrastructure, legal system, greater integration into an emerging global system and secular upswing in prices spawned a strong middle cultivating class-caste at least in some areas.
The need for an english-speaking native bureaucracy bridged the great regional diversity and laid the foundations of Indian nationalism.
Conditions of the lowest tier in the social hierarchy relatively unchanged at best, and possibly worsened  because of greater commercialisation of the economy : Periodic famines.

The British (“Colonial”) Impact - III
The middle class (first urban, and then rural with the advent of Mahatma Gandhi) was the backbone of the anti-imperialist movement.
The anti-imperialist movement riven with internal religious and social dissensions culminating in
Partition of British India along religious divides
Reverse discrimination  structured into the new Constitution, with the scope beingenlarged after Independence.
 Indian Nationalism had a strong economic content (especially ‘drain of wealth’ outside the country and destruction of domestic industry), and also influenced by socialist ideas. These were to greatly influence the policies of Independent India.

The British (“Colonial”) Impact - IV
At Independence (1947)
Drain of wealth out of the Country
No modern agricultural technology
Had been de-industrialised earlier, but the beginnings of modern, especially textile industry, laid.
Beginnings of a modern infrastructure.
Growing poverty and backwardness compounded by  the first stage of the modern demographic cycle after 1921, resulting in (continuing) high population growth rates.
Regional and linguistic diversity led to a federal political structure with strong central powers.
British parliamentary democracy took firm root
India in the forefront of anti-colonial movements in Asia and Africa in the twentieth century, and economically more advanced than most of these countries in 1950
